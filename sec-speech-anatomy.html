    Section 3.1 The Anatomy of Speech Production

    Sound production involves the respiratory system (lungs), the larynx (voice box), and the vocal tract (throat, mouth, and nasal cavity). Understanding these anatomical components is essential for describing how sounds are articulated.
    🔗

    Subsection 3.1.1 Airstream Mechanisms

    Most speech sounds use air flowing from the lungs, but some languages employ other airstream mechanisms:
    🔗
    • Pulmonic egressive.
      Air flows outward from the lungs (used in virtually all languages for normal speech).
      🔗
      🔗
    • Pulmonic ingressive.
      Air flows inward into the lungs. English speakers sometimes use this for affirmative clicking or gasping, but it’s rare in formal phonemes.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Ejectives.
      The vocal cords close, trapping air, which is then released explosively. Common in Caucasian languages (Georgian, Abkhaz) and some African languages (Amharic, Swahili). Examples: [p’], [t’], [k’].
      🔗
      🔗
    • Implosives.
      Air flows inward with a simultaneous closure. Found in many African languages (Sindhi, Zulu). Examples: [ɓ], [ɗ].
      🔗
      🔗
    • Clicks.
      A closure is released suddenly, creating a suction sound. Famous from Khoisan languages of southern Africa. Examples: [ʘ] (bilabial click), [ǀ] (dental click), [!] (palatal click).
      🔗
      🔗

    Remark 3.1.1. Exotic Airstream Mechanisms.

    While English uses only pulmonic egressive airflow, languages like Xhosa (South Africa) employ multiple clicks—up to 20 different click consonants! These sounds, which seem exotic to English speakers, are completely natural and phonologically integrated into Khoisan languages.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.1.2 Voicing and the Vocal Folds

    The vocal folds (vocal cords) are two membranes in the larynx that vibrate when air passes through, producing voicing. The frequency of vibration determines pitch, while the presence or absence of vibration distinguishes voicing (phonologically important).
    🔗

    Definition 3.1.2.

    Voiced sounds are produced with vocal fold vibration ([b], [d], [v], [z], all vowels).
    🔗
    Voiceless sounds are produced without vibration ([p], [t], [f], [s]).
    🔗
    🔗
    You can feel voicing by placing your fingers on your throat while producing [zzzzz] (voiced) versus [ssssss] (voiceless). The vibration is palpable for [z] but absent for [s].
    🔗
    Aspiration refers to a puff of air released after a voiceless stop. In English, [p] in pin is aspirated [pʰ], while [p] in spin is unaspirated [p]. Phonetically distinct, but phonologically the same in English (both are the phoneme /p/). In Mandarin Chinese, however, aspiration is phonologically significant: [pʰ] (aspirated p) and [p] (unaspirated p) represent different phonemes.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.1.3 Place of Articulation

    Place of articulation describes where in the vocal tract a sound is produced. Major places include:
    🔗
    • Bilabial.
      Both lips come together: [p], [b], [m]
      🔗
      🔗
    • Labiodental.
      Lower lip and upper teeth: [f], [v]
      🔗
      🔗
    • Dental.
      Tongue tip against upper teeth: [θ] (as in think), [ð] (as in this). Not found in all languages—many use alveolars instead.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Alveolar.
      Tongue tip against the alveolar ridge (behind upper teeth): [t], [d], [n], [s], [z], [l], [r]
      🔗
      🔗
    • Postalveolar / Palato-alveolar.
      Tongue blade against area just behind alveolar ridge: [ʃ] (as in ship), [ʒ] (as in measure), [tʃ] (as in church), [dʒ] (as in judge)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Retroflex.
      Tongue tip curled back: [ɻ] (American English r), [ʈ], [ɖ]. Common in Arabic, Mandarin, and Indian languages.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Palatal.
      Tongue body against hard palate: [j] (as in yes), [ɲ] (as in Spanish niño)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Velar.
      Tongue body against soft palate (velum): [k], [g], [ŋ] (as in sing)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Uvular.
      Back of tongue against uvula: [q] (as in Arabic), [χ], [ʁ] (as in French). Rare in English.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Pharyngeal.
      Deep in the throat: [ʕ], [ħ]. Found in Arabic and Hebrew.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Glottal.
      At the vocal folds: [ʔ] (glottal stop, as in German beachten), [h]
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.1.4 Manner of Articulation

    Manner of articulation describes how air is modified as it passes through the vocal tract:
    🔗
    • Stops (Plosives).
      Complete closure of the vocal tract: [p], [b], [t], [d], [k], [g], [ʔ]
      🔗
      🔗
    • Fricatives.
      Air flows through a narrow channel, creating friction/turbulence: [f], [v], [θ], [ð], [s], [z], [ʃ], [ʒ], [x], [ɣ], [h]
      🔗
      🔗
    • Affricates.
      Stop followed immediately by a fricative with the same place of articulation: [tʃ] (as in church), [dʒ] (as in judge), [ts] (as in German Zahn)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Nasals.
      Oral closure with air flowing through the nose: [m], [n], [ŋ]. All nasals are voiced.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Approximants (Glides).
      Tongue approaches but doesn’t block: [w] (bilabial-velar), [j] (palatal), [ɥ] (labio-palatal, as in French lui)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Liquids.
      Characterized by tongue position with partial obstruction: [l] (lateral), [r] (rhotic/approximant)
      🔗
      🔗
    Different languages employ different subsets of these manners. English uses stops, fricatives, affricates, nasals, approximants, and liquids. Mandarin Chinese, by contrast, lacks fricatives like [v], [θ], or [ð].
