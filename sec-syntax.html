    Section 1.4 Syntax: Sentence Structure

    Syntax is the study of how words combine into larger structures (phrases and sentences) and the rules governing their arrangement.
    🔗

    Subsection 1.4.1 Word Order

    The most obvious syntactic difference across languages is word order. While all languages can express basic concepts like Agent, Action, and Patient/Object, they order these elements differently:
    🔗
    *German shows both patterns: SVO in main clauses but SOV in subordinate clauses.
    🔗

    Remark 1.4.2. Word Order Typology.

    Linguist Joseph Greenberg demonstrated that word order is not random. Languages that place the Object after the Verb (SVO, VSO) tend to have prepositions (in the house), while languages that place the Object before the Verb (SOV) tend to have postpositions (the house in). These correlations suggest deep structural principles organizing human language.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 1.4.2 Agreement and Government

    Many languages require agreement between words—a grammatical coordination where one word’s form depends on another’s properties.
    🔗

    Example 1.4.3. Spanish Noun-Adjective Agreement.

    In Spanish, adjectives agree with nouns in gender and number:
    🔗
    • el libro rojo (the red book—masculine singular)
      🔗
    • la casa roja (the red house—feminine singular)
      🔗
    • los libros rojos (the red books—masculine plural)
      🔗
    • las casas rojas (the red houses—feminine plural)
      🔗
    English, by contrast, has no agreement: the red book and the red books use the same adjective form.
    🔗
    🔗
    Agreement systems vary dramatically across languages. Some have rich agreement (Russian, Polish, German), others have minimal agreement (English, Mandarin Chinese), and others employ different strategies altogether (for example, classifier systems in many East Asian languages).
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 1.4.3 Recursion and Embedding

    One fundamental feature of all human languages is recursion: the ability to embed phrases and clauses within themselves infinitely. This allows for limitless complexity:
    🔗
    The book [that I read [while sitting [in the café [where I first met you]]]] is wonderful.
    🔗
    This recursive capability—the ability to create infinite complexity from finite means—is considered a core property of human language and may be unique to our species.
