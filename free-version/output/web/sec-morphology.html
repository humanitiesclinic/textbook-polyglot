    Section 1.3 Morphology: Words and Word Structure

    Morphology examines how meaningful units smaller than words (morphemes) combine to form words, and how words are formed and modified.
    🔗

    Subsection 1.3.1 Morphemes: Minimal Meaningful Units

    A morpheme is the smallest unit of language that carries meaning. Morphemes may be:
    🔗

    Definition 1.3.1.

    Free morphemes can stand alone as words (cat, run, happy). Bound morphemes must attach to other morphemes (the prefix un-, the suffix -ed, the plural -s).
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.3.2. Decomposing Words into Morphemes.

    The word unhappiness contains four morphemes:
    🔗
    • un- (prefix, bound)
      🔗
    • happy (root, free)
      🔗
    • -ness (suffix, bound)
      🔗
    The word dogs contains two morphemes: dog (root, free) and -s (plural, bound).
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 1.3.2 Word Formation Processes

    Languages employ different strategies to form new words:
    🔗
    • Affixation.
      Adding prefixes or suffixes: English un- + happy = unhappy; teach + -er = teacher
      🔗
      🔗
    • Compounding.
      Combining two free morphemes: English blackboard, German Donaudampfschifffahrtsgesellschaftskapitän (Danube steamship company captain—note German’s preference for long compounds)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Derivation.
      Using morphemes to create new word forms with different meanings: happy → happiness, modern → modernize
      🔗
      🔗
    • Inflection.
      Modifying words for grammatical function: English walk → walks, walked, walking
      🔗
      🔗
    • Conversion.
      Changing a word’s part of speech without adding morphemes: English to google (verb from noun), a run (noun from verb)
      🔗
      🔗

    Remark 1.3.3. Agglutination vs. Fusion.

    Some languages, like Turkish and Finnish, are agglutinative, meaning morphemes line up visibly in sequence. Others, like English and Spanish, show fusion, where morpheme boundaries blur through sound changes. Turkish evlerimden (from our houses) shows clear morpheme boundaries: ev-ler-im-den (house-plural-our-from), while English went fuses the past tense morpheme obscurely with the root.
