    Section 3.2 Consonant Inventories Across Languages

    The world’s languages employ vastly different sets of consonants. While English has about 24 consonant phonemes, languages range from Hawaiian (with 8) to Taa (a Khoisan language with over 100).

    Subsection 3.2.2 Consonant Variation Across Languages

    Languages vary dramatically in which consonants they include:
    🔗
    • Hawaiian.
      One of the simplest inventories: [p], [k], [ʔ], [m], [n], [w], [l], [h] (8 consonants). Hawaiian phonotactics also prevents most consonant clusters.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Spanish.
      Approximately 19 consonants, including the interdental fricative [θ] (in Spain) and the palatal lateral [ʎ].
      🔗
      🔗
    • Russian.
      Approximately 36 consonants (counting palatalized and non-palatalized pairs as distinct). Russian systematically contrasts palatalized vs. non-palatalized consonants: [l] vs. [lʲ], [n] vs. [nʲ].
      🔗
      🔗
    • !Xóõ (Khoisan language).
      A Khoisan language with approximately 123 consonant phonemes, including many clicks, ejectives, and other exotic articulations.
      🔗
      🔗

    Remark 3.2.2. Rare Consonants in English.

    Many consonants found in the world’s languages are absent from English: the interdental fricatives [θ] and [ð] are rare (found mainly in English and Icelandic); uvular consonants [q] and [χ] appear in Qatari Arabic and German; pharyngeal consonants [ʕ] and [ħ] appear in Arabic and Hebrew; and clicks are used phonemically only in Khoisan languages and a few Bantu languages of southern Africa.
