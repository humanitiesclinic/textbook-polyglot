    Section 1.2 Phonology: The Sound System

    Phonology is the study of how sounds function in a language. Unlike phonetics (the study of how sounds are physically produced and perceived), phonology examines how sounds are organized and used meaningfully.
    🔗

    Subsection 1.2.1 Phonemes and Allophones

    A phoneme is the smallest unit of sound that makes a difference in meaning. For example, in English, the sounds [p] and [b] are different phonemes because changing one to the other changes the meaning: [pɪn] (pin) versus [bɪn] (bin).
    🔗
    Not every pronunciation difference signals a phoneme difference. Allophones are variant pronunciations of the same phoneme that do not change meaning. English speakers pronounce the [l] in light differently from the [l] in milk (one is “clear l,” the other “dark l”), but these differences don’t matter—[laɪt] and [mɪlk] are understood as containing the same phoneme /l/.
    🔗

    Example 1.2.1. English Vowel Phonemes.

    English has approximately 14-20 vowel phonemes (depending on dialect). Here are some key contrastive pairs:
    🔗
    • [i] as in fleece vs. [ɪ] as in kit
      🔗
    • [e] as in dress vs. [æ] as in trap
      🔗
    • [ɑ] as in lot vs. [ɔ] as in thought
      🔗
    • [u] as in goose vs. [ʊ] as in foot
      🔗
    🔗
    Different languages have different inventories of phonemes. Spanish, for example, has fewer vowel phonemes than English (typically 5), while Khoekhoe (a language spoken in southern Africa) has more than 100 phonemic consonants.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 1.2.2 Distinctive Features

    Phonemes themselves can be broken down into distinctive features—properties that differentiate sounds. Common features include:
    🔗
    • Voicing.
      Whether the vocal cords vibrate ([b] is voiced; [p] is voiceless)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Place of Articulation.
      Where in the vocal tract the obstruction occurs (bilabial [p], alveolar [t], velar [k])
      🔗
      🔗
    • Manner of Articulation.
      How airflow is modified (stops, fricatives, nasals, approximants)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Nasality.
      Whether air flows through the nose ([m] is nasal; [b] is oral)
      🔗
      🔗

    Remark 1.2.2. Why Distinctive Features Matter.

    Distinctive features allow us to see patterns across languages. For instance, many languages have constraints on which sound combinations are permitted. English allows [str] at the beginning of words (string, strap) but not [zdr]. This is because [s] and [z] share features, but differ in voicing, and English doesn’t allow a voiceless fricative before a voiced stop in word-initial position.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 1.2.3 Phonotactics: Sound Combination Rules

    Every language has rules about which sounds can occur together. These rules, called phonotactic constraints, differ across languages:
    🔗
    • English allows [ng] at the end of words (sing, ring) but not at the beginning: *[ngɪp] is not a valid English word.
      🔗
    • Mandarin Chinese does not allow consonant clusters at the beginning of words, so foreign words like street must be adapted (街 jiē includes a vowel).
      🔗
    • Hawaiian has very few allowed consonant clusters, making the language’s phonotactics quite restrictive.
      🔗
    Phonotactic constraints help explain why certain words sound “un-English” even if they contain only English sounds. The word [zɡɛt] (containing perfectly normal English sounds) sounds strange to English speakers because [zg] is not an allowed combination in English phonotactics.
    🔗
